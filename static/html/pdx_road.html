Portland Roadside Architecture

What is Roadside Architecture?

Roadside Architecture is a building that becomes the sign for a business it houses. There are three categories: Thematic, Iconic, and Whimsical.

Thematic

Thematic Roadside Architecture borrows styles from another time or culture. Thematic styles may be from a single culture or a blending of many. The goal is to create a unique experience through the architecture that marries commercial interests with travelogues. Do you know a building that fits this category? Let us know! Send us a picture or link

Iconic

Iconic Roadside Architecture results when the public recognizes a building that is so unique it becomes the image of the business. Either by intent or accident, the building becomes a logo, instantly connecting product to architecture. Do you know a building that fits this category? Let us know! Send us a picture or link.

Whimsical

Whimsical Roadside Architecture uses a common object to form a building or part of a building. The object may represent the business, but not always. This is the most delightful of Roadside works. To be Whimsical, the object does not need to form the entire building, but can simply be incorporated into the design. Do you know a building that fits this category? Let us know! Send us a picture or link.

Waddle's was designed in 1945 by Portland's most famous architect, Pietro Belluschi. A serious study of modern design principles, it became an endearing roadside stop thanks to the name, sign, clock and duck.

The cute duck logo helped make Waddle's a roadside icon.

Waddle's: Halfway between Portland and the Couv. Or is it halfway between Eureka and Canada? Either way, it was a yummy rest stop!

Note the imagery for Jantzen Beach when it was an amusement park. Anyone remember the Big Dipper? It was a wood framed roller coaster that loosened your teeth!

Yaws "Top Notch" restaurant had a number of smaller locations in the Hollywood area. They opened their iconic building at NE 39th and Tillamook in 1957. Ironically the site is now home to a McDonalds's.

Yaw's was known for their wonderfully unique hamburgers, French fries and gravy, and coconut cream pie. Remember the "WSGOO" on the menu? In reference to their fresh burgers it meant We Still Grind Our Own!

The "Tootsie Roll Cop" handed out tootise rolls to all the kids. It was a nice way for a beat patrol officer to connect with teenagers and to monitor any possible shenanigans happening inside the cars.

A Yaw's fun fact: In the 1920's Mr. E.E. Franz and W.P. Yaw invented the hamburger bun!

The Yaw's Top Notch menu was an icon all by itself!

Yaws in its golden age.

This restored Signal Gas Station is at 8320 N. Lombard in St. Johns. It was restored by Rob Phillips of Renovation Properties and is a wonderful example of adapting old icons to new uses.

Old Signal Gas Stations can be found in various conditions of "deferred maintenance" around Portland awaiting patiently for new life.

Rob Phillips of Renovation Properties found the Signal Gas Company specification book from the 1930's to assist in the detail oriented and lovingly accurate restoration.

This iconic gas station is now a pizzeria and is a distinctive landmark for the business.

The neon really brings the beautiful Art Deco lines into focus.

Let's hope this project is just one of many lovingly restored works of Iconic Roadside Architecture.

Safeway has had a number of prototypes. Their gull wing concept was immediately recognized as a Safeway. The night view really highlights its wonderful form.

The original Mickey D's prototype was first built in 1953. It is so unique, it is instantly recognizable as a McDonald's and was very artfully conceived.

McDonalds opened their 500th prototype in 1967 in Toledo, Ohio. This example is on SE Powell at 91st and is Portland's only remaining original McD.

Although no longer a drive-in, this iconic building is still used for special events such as birthday parties. A bigger restaurant was built next door.

The pagoda style roof of the old Hollywood Library actually had a purpose other than establishing an iconic presence. Can you guess? The original boiler exhaust was routed from the basement up to the center top roof form. If you look closely, you can still see the vent screens. Forget form following function. This was function being shoved into form. But, we like it!

The old Hollywood Library's generous glass walls connect the inside with the outside. Now the building is home to Fluer De Lis Bakery, and Wee Works Daycare.

A new door in the existing window wall fits in with the original pattern. The black aluminum frame offers a nice contrast to the original silver colored framing. Thank you Perkins Architectural.

The Broadway Veterinary Clinic by Perkins Architectural was intended by the owner and architect to create an iconic image for the business. Since the mid 2000's it sits on NE Broadway at 24th where Broadway widens to 4 lanes.

The Broadway Veterinary Clinic's angular roof brings natural light to key interior spaces. Perhaps flamboyant, it is not capricious. If the building becomes an icon it will be not be determined by the architect, but by the general public.

The Bomber Gas Station in Milwaukie, Oregon. Art Lacey flew the B-17 from an Air Force Base in 1947. That is Art, the proud pilot and owner.

When Art Lacey did his test flight at the Oklahoma Air Base, he needed a co-pilot, but he had traveled alone. So, he put a mannequin in the co-pilot's seat and took off. Since the co-pilot is the only person who can lower the landing gear, Art crashed the plane and also destroyed another one on the ground in the process. The Air Force wrote it off as wind damage.

When Art took off across the country (With a real co-pilot this time) he ran into a snow storm between Palm Springs and Klamath Falls. He dropped down under the clouds to follow the rail road tracks so he could navigate. While following the railroad tracks in the snow storm, the co-pilot was in the nose cone and would shout "Tunnel!" whenever a tunnel was spotted so Art could gain altitude and go over the mountain!

When Art piloted his the Bomber from Oklahoma, he landed his B-17 at the Troutdale Airport, but could not obtain a permit to transport the plane to Milwaukie. So, late one Saturday evening, he just drove it down the highway. All he received was a $10 ticket for an oversize load. Quite the bargain!

The bomber is currently being refurbished. Donations are always appreciated.

The Bomber in its hey day. The Bomber was more than simply a gas station. It was a landmark destination.

The Coon Chicken originated in Salt Lake City. In the 1930's the restaurant expanded to Portland and Seattle. The building is at 52nd and Sandy Boulevard and houses Cylde's Prime Rib.

It was not intended to be racist. The smiling winking face was created to attract children and families, and was featured on the front door, menus, and every paper product. These are highly collectible and knock-offs abound so be careful.

1929 Dutch Village service station on Terwilliger near Bertha. All the buildings were representational of the theme, but the windmill is the memorable and whimsical feature.

The unique Shoe Service company was located on NE 21st on Sandy Blvd. They repaired shoes and garments. Oscar's Silk Hat on MLK (Union Avenue) south of Killingsworth. Although the whimsical feature is only over the front door, it creates a memorable image for the entire building.

The silk hat formed the shape of the menu.

Whiskey Barrel Diner on 82nd Avenue. A McDonalds now stands in its place. The barrels were actually promoting Triple X Root Beer and served as a drive-in.

Steigerwald Dairy built in 1926 at NE 37th and Sandy Blvd. The dairy occupied the building for about 10 years. For awhile in the 1930s, a luncheonette occupied a space in the buildng and they naturally called their business "The Milk Bottle Luncheonette."

The old Steigerwald Dairy's milk bottle was covered up by a cylinder and a 7-Up sign was placed on the top. If you look closely though the slotted windows, you can see the shadow of the original milk bottle which still remains.

Even though a Budweiser and then other business signs have replaced the 7-Up sign several years ago, people still refer to the building as "The 7-Up Building".

Originally the Orange Blossom Luncheonette built in 1928 on NE 75th and Sandy Boulevard was a roadside stop for hungry travelers. The vertical stripes were originally windows, but are now covered.

Sandy Boulevard was once the main highway drawing travelers from the east into Portland. This whimsical work caught their attention and became a memorable respite.

Due to the well executed proportions, the building became known as the "Sandy Jug" for clearly obvious reasons.

Successful whimsies pay close attention to the details as pictured here. Achieving the correct proportions and accuracy of the original object are essential.

Currently the "Pirate's Cove", the building will always be known by locals as the "Sandy Jug".

So, let us hope the owner returns the "Sandy Jug" to its original luncheonette glory.
